Why a CV is not enough to evaluate a high-impact candidate

A resume describes the past. It does not predict behaviour under pressure, nor the decision risk a candidate carries with them.

What matters is not on the page.

The CV as a marketing tool, not an evaluation instrument

A curriculum vitae is a document optimised to create a favourable impression. It is not — and has never been — a tool for objective evaluation. Every professional who knows the rules of the game learns to select, emphasise and contextualise their experience in light of the role they are targeting.

This is not dishonesty — it is communicative competence. The problem is that many organisations continue to use the CV as a primary filter in executive selection, delegating to a self-referential document a decision worth millions of euros in potential impact.

What the CV cannot measure

For high-impact roles — C-suite, VP, board member — the critical variables are not the ones that appear on a sheet of paper. They are behavioural, cognitive and contextual variables that only emerge under specific conditions:

  • Decision-making capacity under pressure — How do they react when data is incomplete, time is limited and consequences are irreversible?
  • Organisational conflict management — Can they navigate power dynamics without destroying them? Can they hold uncomfortable positions before the board?
  • Adaptation to specific context — Excellent performance in a scale-up context can be a negative predictor in a mature corporate environment. And vice versa.
  • Accountability patterns — In past failures, what was their real role? How did they narrate, process and integrate them?
  • Managerial ego solidity — Can they distinguish between their personal interest and the organisation's interest when they diverge?
Realistic scenario

A company hires a Commercial Director to lead a growth phase. The CV is impeccable. International experience. Prestigious companies. Positive references.

Within a few months, however, problems emerge that no CV could have revealed: difficulty managing conflict, inability to delegate, tensions with management, loss of team trust, decision stalling.

The technical competencies were real.

The decision risk was elsewhere.

By the time it surfaces, the hire has already been made.

The traditional interview: an illusion of knowledge

A CV is almost always accompanied by an interview process that — in its traditional form — has very limited predictive power. Unstructured interviews, dominated by subjective impression, tend primarily to measure how skilled the candidate is at being interviewed. Not how suited they are for the role.

The phenomenon is well-documented in organisational behaviour research: it is called affinity bias. The evaluator tends to rate positively those who communicate in a similar style, share cultural references, or know how to "read the room" during an interview. All useful qualities — but not necessarily predictive of the critical performance we expect.

Towards a multi-structure evaluation

The answer is not to eliminate the CV or the interview. It is to integrate these tools into a broader evaluation framework, where each component measures a specific dimension and results are weighted explicitly.

A structured assessment system for executive roles typically includes:

  • Analysis of behavioural evidence from documented scenarios (advanced STAR method)
  • Decision simulations in controlled ambiguity contexts
  • Structured — not informal — reference checks with selected interlocutors
  • Cognitive profile mapping and tolerance-to-uncertainty assessment
  • Analysis of narrative consistency across multiple sources

This is not a slow process. It is a precise process — and the difference between the two is exactly the distance between a defensible decision and an expensive gamble.

Evaluation Stress Test

If we removed the CV

If we removed this candidate's CV and kept only:

→ Observed behaviours
→ Decisions made
→ Reactions under pressure
→ Narrative consistency
→ Risk signals

would we still hire them?

If the answer is no, the CV is driving the decision too much.

The question every decision maker should ask

Before making an offer to an executive candidate, the question is not: "Are we convinced?" — Absolute certainty does not exist in human decisions. The question is: "Can we explain, with data in hand, why this candidate is the most robust choice among those evaluated?"

If the answer reduces to "they have an excellent CV and made a great impression", the evaluation process is not yet solid enough to bear the weight of the decision.
