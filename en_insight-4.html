Why the Best Hiring Decisions Don't Start with the Candidate

The most effective organisations don't try to eliminate risk. They seek to reduce avoidable errors.

CL
Cristiano Ludovisi Founder & CEO, RATIO Decision Intelligence
Profile
The decision begins before the candidate.

The problem starts before the candidate

For years I watched companies invest enormous amounts of time and energy searching for the "perfect candidate". Impeccable CVs, thorough interviews and increasingly sophisticated tools.

Yet many of the most costly decisions continued to turn into avoidable mistakes.

Over time I understood that the problem rarely lies with the candidate.

The real problem starts much earlier.

It starts with questions that no one tends to ask.

The questions no one asks
The problem Do we truly know what problem this person will need to solve?
Success Have we defined precisely what success looks like in that role?
Context Is there genuine alignment between the organisational context and the expectations we've set?
Risk What would the consequences of a wrong decision actually be?

Reducing avoidable errors — not eliminating risk

The strongest organisations don't try to eliminate risk. They seek to reduce avoidable errors.

That is why RATIO was not built as a system to evaluate people.

It was built as a system to protect decisions.

When a position carries high organisational impact, the quality of the information that precedes the choice matters more than the intuition that accompanies the interview.

46% Of new executives fail within their first 18 months — for non-technical reasons
3–15× Annual salary: average cost of a C-suite hiring mistake (source: DDI)
18 months Average time before the problem becomes clearly visible in the organisation

These numbers tell a consistent story: the real cost of an executive error doesn't surface immediately. It emerges slowly, in the form of delayed decisions, departing talent and missed opportunities — long before anyone officially acknowledges it as a mistake.

The wrong starting point

Most executive selection processes begin with an obvious question: "Who are we looking for?"

It's the wrong question.

The right question is: "What risk are we trying to manage?"

This distinction changes everything. It changes the evaluation criteria. It changes the interview questions. It changes the weight attributed to different pieces of evidence. And above all, it changes the quality of the final output — the decision.

The process that produces defensible decisions

PHASE 1
Risk definition
Before searching for a candidate, clarify which error you want to avoid and what the consequences would be.
PHASE 2
Explicit criteria
Evaluation criteria are defined before meeting candidates — not during. Success is made measurable.
PHASE 3
Structured analysis
Objective data is separated from subjective perception. The decision profile is mapped with method.
PHASE 4
Defensible output
The final decision is documented, traceable and justifiable to the board, investors and to yourself.

When risk is clear, the choice becomes defensible

The best decisions don't start with the candidate.

They start with understanding the risk.

And when the risk is clear, the choice becomes more defensible — to the board, to investors, and to yourself in the months that follow.

This is the difference between a traditional selection process and a decision intelligence system applied to executive hiring. It's not about more sophisticated tools. It's about a different starting point.

Decision Stress Test

Before launching your next executive selection process, answer this question:

Do we know precisely which error we are trying to avoid?

If the answer is not immediate and shared by all decision makers involved, the process already has a critical risk point — before you've even looked at the first CV.

Question for the Board
If you were to launch a C-suite selection process today, could you formulate in a single precise sentence what organisational risk you are seeking to reduce with that hire?

If the answer takes more than thirty seconds, the risk point is not the candidate.
It's the process.
