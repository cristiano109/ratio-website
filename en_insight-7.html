The Most Dangerous Assumption in Executive Hiring

Every executive hiring decision begins with assumptions. Organisations that recognise them make better decisions. Those that don't often pay for invisible mistakes.

The decision begins long before the candidate enters the room.

The decision does not begin in the interview room. It begins weeks earlier, in a quiet moment when someone first reads a name, glances at a title, or hears a recommendation from a trusted colleague. In that instant, a hypothesis is formed. And from that moment forward, everything that follows is shaped — often invisibly — by that first assumption.

This is not a flaw in human judgment. It is how the brain works. Faced with complexity and uncertainty, the mind does not suspend evaluation. It accelerates it. It builds a provisional model of reality and then seeks evidence to confirm it. The process is efficient. It is also, in executive hiring, frequently dangerous.

Most organisations understand that hiring decisions carry risk. What fewer recognise is that the most consequential risk does not arise from a flawed interview technique or a poorly designed assessment process. It arises from assumptions that were never examined — assumptions that quietly structured the entire decision before the first conversation took place.

The Moment the Decision Is Actually Made

Before a candidate enters the room, the organisation has already built a detailed mental portrait. The CV has been read. A school has been noted. A previous employer has triggered an association. A job title has implied a level of authority. A referral from a respected source has conferred credibility. None of this is neutral.

Each of these signals activates a set of assumptions — about capability, about cultural fit, about trajectory, about risk. These assumptions are not random. They are drawn from experience, from pattern recognition, from shared organisational beliefs about what good leadership looks like. That is precisely what makes them feel credible. And precisely what makes them difficult to challenge.

The language a candidate uses in a first exchange shapes a perception of intelligence and precision. Physical presence and confidence generate impressions of authority and resilience. A history of visibility — published work, board roles, media appearances — creates an assumption of influence. The reputation of a former employer becomes a proxy for the candidate's own quality. All of these are assumptions. Very few are facts.

The problem is not that they are formed. The problem is that they are rarely made visible. When assumptions remain implicit, they cannot be tested. When they cannot be tested, they cannot be corrected. And when they cannot be corrected, they govern the outcome.

The hiring decision is not made during the interview. It is made the first time someone forms an opinion about the candidate — and that opinion is built entirely on assumptions.

How Assumptions Become Decision Risk

The transition from assumption to risk follows a well-documented cognitive path. Once a favourable initial impression has been formed, the brain engages in what behavioural scientists call confirmation bias — the tendency to seek, interpret, and retain information in ways that confirm what one already believes. In a hiring context, this means that a strong early impression does not simply influence the final decision. It actively shapes every subsequent observation.

Questions in the interview are unconsciously framed to elicit confirmation. Hesitations in the candidate's answers are interpreted charitably. Stronger signals from other candidates are minimised. The evaluation process, designed to generate information, becomes instead a mechanism for validating a conclusion that was reached before it began.

Related to this is the halo effect — the tendency for a single positive attribute to cast a favourable light over all other qualities. A candidate who is articulate, well-presented, and composed is routinely assumed to be also analytically rigorous, operationally effective, and culturally aligned. These are entirely distinct competencies. They share no necessary relationship. Yet the assumption that they travel together is pervasive and powerful.

There is also the question of decision framing — the way in which the problem itself is defined before evaluation begins. When an organisation frames a vacancy as a search for someone who can "drive growth," it has already embedded a set of assumptions about what the role requires. It has made certain profiles visible and others invisible. The quality of every subsequent decision is constrained by the quality of that initial framing.

Realistic Scenario

A technology company in international expansion seeks a new Chief Revenue Officer. The ideal profile is built around an assumption never explicitly formulated: that the right CRO must come from a comparable-scale SaaS company. The selected candidate has an impeccable track record — having driven growth from 50 to 200 million ARR in four years. The board is unanimous. The decision is made with high confidence.

Fourteen months later, results are significantly below expectations. Post-decision analysis reveals that the central assumption — that the previous scale was comparable — was never verified. The target market had completely different sales cycle dynamics. The competencies that had generated results in the previous context were not transferable to the current one.

The assumption was not unreasonable. It was simply never verified. And the cost of not making it explicit had been transferred to the organisation.

The Five Most Dangerous Assumptions in Executive Hiring

In the analysis of high-stakes executive selection processes, five categories of assumptions recur with systematic frequency and produce the most significant damage when they prove incorrect. They are not the only dangerous assumptions. But they are the ones that appear most consistently in decision failures that could have been prevented.

Previous Scale = Future Capability
Having led a large organisation is interpreted as a reliable predictor of the ability to do so in a different context. In reality, scale measures exposure, not transferability. A leader who excels in a structured environment may be entirely misplaced in a chaotic one — and vice versa.
Employer Brand = Candidate Quality
Coming from a prestigious company automatically transfers credibility to the candidate. This assumption ignores a fundamental variable: individual contribution within a successful organisation is rarely separable from the systemic context that made that success possible. The brand certifies access, not performance.
Confidence = Decision Quality
Candidates who express themselves with certainty are perceived as more capable of leadership. Research on decision-making suggests the opposite: the ability to sustain uncertainty and formulate explicitly provisional hypotheses is a more reliable indicator of decision quality in complex contexts. Confidence may be a communication style, not analytical depth.
Cultural Affinity = Adaptability
A person with traits culturally similar to the evaluating team is perceived as better suited to the context. This assumption confuses familiarity with the capacity for adaptation — which are opposite qualities. Cultural familiarity reduces initial friction but does not predict performance under conditions of organisational stress or deep change.
The Process Is Objective
This is perhaps the most entrenched assumption. The organisation presumes that its evaluation process generates objective data. In reality, every phase — from profile definition to evaluation criteria — is built on implicit assumptions that determine which information is collected and how it is interpreted.

Why More Data Does Not Solve the Problem

The conventional organisational response to decision risk is the accumulation of information. More interview rounds are added, psychometric tests are introduced, references are multiplied. The belief is that more data necessarily produces a better decision.

This belief is itself an assumption — and for many high-complexity contexts, it is an incorrect one.

Research on decision-making under complexity reveals a counterintuitive phenomenon: beyond a critical threshold, the accumulation of information does not improve decision quality. It increases the confidence of decision makers — who develop growing trust in their own evaluation — without proportionally increasing the accuracy of that evaluation. More data, same decision quality, more confidence. It is a particularly dangerous combination.

The problem is not the quantity of data collected. The problem is the quality of the questions guiding its collection and interpretation. And the quality of the questions depends on the ability to make explicit the assumptions governing the decision process.

What Distinguishes a High-Quality Decision

It would be a misreading of this analysis to conclude that intuition has no place in executive hiring. It does. Experienced judgment — the kind that accumulates through years of observing how leaders behave under pressure, how they manage uncertainty, how they build and lose credibility — carries genuine diagnostic value.

The problem is not that organisations trust their judgment. The problem is that they rarely subject it to scrutiny. A high-quality decision does not eliminate intuition. It disciplines it. The goal is not to replace human judgment with a formula or a score. It is to make that judgment verifiable — to expose the assumptions on which it rests, to test them against available evidence, and to identify where confidence is warranted and where it is not.

This requires a deliberate shift in how the process is structured. It means beginning not with the candidate, but with a rigorous analysis of the decision itself — what the role genuinely demands, what context the incoming leader will operate within, what assumptions are embedded in the existing picture of success, and where those assumptions are most vulnerable to error.

Decision Stress Test

Before the Next Executive Selection Process

Ask these questions before meeting the first candidate.

→ What are the three main assumptions underlying our ideal profile for this role?
→ Which of these assumptions derive from the current context and which from the context in which we succeeded in the past?
→ If our assumption about the ideal profile were wrong, how would the process change?
→ Who in our process has the explicit mandate to challenge the dominant assumptions?
→ What specific evidence would allow us to falsify our hypothesis about the ideal candidate?

If you cannot answer these questions with precision, the process already has a critical risk point — regardless of the quality of the candidates you will meet.

This is not a candidate problem. It is a decision architecture problem.

The Difference Between Managing Risk and Suffering It

There is a systematic difference between organisations that consistently produce high-quality executive hiring decisions and those that generate costly errors with frequency. This difference does not reside primarily in the resources available, nor in the quality of the candidates accessible. It resides in an epistemic practice: the ability to identify and explicitly verify the assumptions governing the decision process.

Organisations that actively manage their decision risk have developed mechanisms to do exactly this. Some through dedicated roles — figures with the explicit mandate to challenge dominant assumptions in the process. Others through structured practices: decision pre-mortems in which participants are asked to imagine that the decision has proved wrong and to identify the assumptions that would have led to that result.

What unites these approaches is a fundamental awareness: the risk does not reside in the candidates encountered. It resides in the beliefs with which they are evaluated.

Two Approaches to Decision Risk in Executive Hiring

APPROACH A
Profile definition by analogy
The ideal profile is built by replicating characteristics of those who succeeded in the past. Assumptions about context transferability are never verified.
OUTCOME A
Systematic confirmation bias
The process selects candidates who confirm pre-existing assumptions. Contextual risk remains invisible until it manifests as an error.
APPROACH B
Critical assumption mapping
Before meeting candidates, assumptions about the role and context are made explicit. The process is designed to verify or disconfirm them.
OUTCOME B
Epistemically defensible decision
The decision can be justified not only with the data collected, but with the questions that guided the collection. Verifiable, reproducible, resistant to revision.

Question for the Board
In the last executive selection process conducted by your organisation: are you able to list the three main assumptions on which the ideal profile was based, and to indicate what specific evidence you collected to verify each of them before meeting candidates?

If the answer is no, the process was built on cognitive ground that no one had verified.
This is not a selection problem. It is a decision method problem.
