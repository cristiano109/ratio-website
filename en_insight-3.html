How to reduce decision risk in strategic executive hiring

Strategic hiring cannot rely on intuition alone. Structured methodologies exist to reduce error to measurable, defensible data.

The quality of a decision is in the process behind it.

The paradox of trusting intuition

The most capable leaders know how to make fast decisions under uncertainty. This competency is real — and valuable. But it becomes a risk when applied indiscriminately to hiring decisions, where confirmation bias, the halo effect and time pressure combine in a particularly insidious way.

The paradox of managerial intuition is this: the better a leader is at deciding, the more confident they are in their ability to evaluate people — and the less likely they are to subject their choices to a structured verification process. The result is that the most costly hiring mistakes are often made by highly competent decision makers.

82% Of CEOs declare having made at least one wrong strategic hire in the last 3 years
67% Of these attribute the mistake to an "instinctive" evaluation not supported by data
4× Higher probability of success with a structured selection process (McKinsey)

The numbers point in one clear direction: unsupported intuition increases risk, it does not reduce it. And the cost of an executive mistake rarely stops at replacement.

Five principles of a low-risk selection process

There is no selection process that eliminates risk. There is a process that measures, distributes and makes it manageable. These are the five fundamental principles:

1. Define risk before meeting candidates. Every role has specific contextual risk factors: the organisation's current moment, the complexity of internal relationships, expected operational challenges. Before even opening the search, the decision team should answer: "What behaviours, in this context, could lead to failure?" The answer becomes the risk map to monitor during evaluation.

2. Separate technical competency evaluation from decision profile assessment. Technical competencies are verified with objective methods. The decision profile requires different tools: scenarios, cases, simulations. Conflating the two — or using the traditional interview to assess both — is one of the primary causes of error.

3. Structure the scoring process before evaluation, not after. Criteria weights must be decided before meeting candidates, not adjusted retrospectively in light of impressions received. This drastically reduces confirmation bias.

"A robust selection process is not slower. It is more precise. And precision is the only form of respect for what is at stake."

4. Insert an independent review point. Those involved in the selection inevitably develop an orientation towards the candidate they have evaluated. An external review — even partial — of the process and collected evidence significantly increases the quality of the final decision.

5. Document the reasoning, not just the choice. Traceability of the decision-making process is an investment, not a cost. If in the following months difficulty signals emerge, having documented the reasons for the choice makes it possible to distinguish between an evaluation error and an unforeseen contextual evolution — and to learn from the difference.

When to activate a structured process

Not every hire requires an assessment at this level. The criteria for activating a structured selection process are essentially three:

  • The role has direct impact on the organisation's strategic decisions
  • The cost of an error — direct or indirect — significantly exceeds the cost of the evaluation process
  • The operational context is complex, ambiguous or undergoing significant change

If at least two of these criteria are met, relying on a structured process is not an additional cost — it is the most efficient form of risk management available.

The decision as a responsible act

Ultimately, the quality of a hiring decision is not measured only by its outcome. It is measured by the quality of the process that produced it. A decision made with method, data and awareness of the risks is a defensible decision — regardless of outcome. This is the kind of decision a responsible leader can sustain before the board, investors, and — above all — themselves.

“
The best decisions do not eliminate risk.
They make it visible before it becomes a problem.

A measured risk can be managed. An invisible risk cannot.

The question is not whether risk exists in the decision.

The question is whether that risk was measured before deciding.
