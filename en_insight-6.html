Wrong Decisions Don't Come from Insufficient Information. They Come from Wrong Assumptions.

Every important decision rests on dozens of invisible assumptions. The more flawed they are, the greater the risk. Decision Intelligence exists, first and foremost, to make them visible.

Assumptions are the silent architecture of every decision.

Each time an organisation faces a strategic decision, data collection occupies the centre of the process. Dashboards are built, analyses are commissioned, reports multiply. The implicit idea is that the decision problem is an information problem: if we had enough data, the right answer would emerge on its own.

This idea is comforting. And it is almost always incomplete.

The reality that emerges from analysing high-impact decision failures — in executive hiring, strategic choices, leadership assessments — tells a different story. Wrong decisions rarely arise from a shortage of information. They arise from wrong assumptions about that information. They arise from unverified beliefs that the mind treats as facts. From interpretations taken for granted that no one has ever challenged.

The distinction is not semantic. It is the distance between two entirely different types of risk, with profoundly different implications for anyone responsible for high-stakes decisions.

The great misconception: we believe we are reasoning about facts

There is a fundamental distinction that three decades of decision-making research has established — and that organisations continue to systematically ignore: the distinction between data, interpretations, and assumptions.

Data are verifiable observations. A candidate has worked fifteen years at a multinational. The department's revenue grew 23% over the past three years. The team's annual turnover rate is 8%. These are facts.

Interpretations are the inferences the mind constructs automatically from those facts. "She has worked fifteen years in a large organisation, so she has experience managing complexity." "Revenue grew 23%, so the CFO has done a good job." "Turnover is low, so the team is solid." These are interpretations — probable inferences, but not certainties.

Assumptions are something subtler still: the implicit premises that underpin those interpretations. They are the beliefs the mind considers so obvious that they are never explicitly formulated. This is where decision risk concentrates in its most dangerous form.

When a decision-maker reasons about an executive candidate, they are not reasoning about the facts of a CV. They are reasoning through a dense system of invisible assumptions: that past experience predicts future behaviour, that the previous context is comparable to the current one, that skills demonstrated in one environment are transferable to another. None of these assumptions is necessarily false. But none is automatically true. And almost none is ever explicitly verified.

We do not make decisions based on facts. We make decisions based on our interpretations of facts — which in turn rest on assumptions we have never explicitly formulated.

What assumptions are and why the brain creates them

In the language of Decision Intelligence, an assumption is a belief that the cognitive system treats as true without having verified it. It is not a hypothesis — which implies awareness of uncertainty. It is not knowledge — which implies supporting evidence. It is something more insidious: an implicit certainty operating below the decision-maker's threshold of awareness.

The brain produces them continuously, and it does so for structurally adaptive reasons. In a complex decision environment, processing every piece of information from scratch would require cognitive resources that simply do not exist. The cognitive system therefore constructs shortcuts — pre-built meaning structures that allow rapid navigation of complex situations by applying already-elaborated patterns.

This capacity underlies much of human intelligence. The problem emerges when these shortcuts are applied in contexts that are superficially similar but structurally different from those in which the patterns were originally learned. In these cases, assumptions do not accelerate reasoning: they distort it.

Three forces systematically amplify this effect in organisational settings.

The first is time pressure. When decisions must be made quickly, the cognitive system relies even more heavily on pre-built assumptions, further reducing space for explicit verification.

The second is hierarchical authority. In organisations, the assumptions held by the highest-ranking decision-maker tend to acquire a quasi-normative status: they are rarely challenged by other process participants, regardless of their epistemic quality.

The third is domain familiarity. Decision-makers with the deepest expertise in a field tend to develop a particularly rich system of pre-built assumptions — which makes them fast and confident, but also systematically exposed to the risk of applying patterns learned in contexts that have ceased to be relevant.

Realistic scenario

A board evaluates two candidates for the CEO role. Candidate A has led a €800 million division at a listed multinational. Candidate B has built a company from scratch that reached €120 million before being acquired. Consensus forms quickly around Candidate A: "He has managed organisations ten times the size. Experience speaks for itself." The underlying assumption is never explicitly formulated, yet it governs the entire dynamic: the scale of the previous organisation is a reliable proxy for the quality of future decision-making. No one asks whether the markets are comparable. No one explores what "led" means in a multinational structure with 200 staff members versus building a company with six people. The assumption is not verified. It becomes the decision.

The assumption was not wrong in absolute terms. It was wrong for that specific context. No one had asked the right question.

The five most dangerous assumptions in executive decisions

In the analysis of high-impact decision processes, five categories of assumptions emerge with systematic frequency and produce the most significant damage when they prove incorrect. They are not the only dangerous assumptions. But they appear with the greatest regularity in decision failures that could have been prevented.

Experience = Competence
Having performed a role for many years is automatically interpreted as mastery of that role. In reality, experience measures the quantity of exposure, not the quality of learning. One can perform the same role for twenty years applying the same flawed patterns every time.
Confidence = Leadership
Candidates who speak with assurance, who do not hesitate, who show no doubt, are perceived as more capable of leadership. Research on decision-making suggests the opposite: the ability to sustain uncertainty, to formulate explicitly provisional hypotheses, is a more reliable indicator of decision quality in complex contexts.
Cultural fit = Adaptability
A person with cultural traits similar to those of the evaluating team is perceived as better suited to the context. This assumption conflates cultural affinity with adaptive capacity — which is precisely the opposite. Cultural familiarity reduces initial friction but does not predict performance under organisational stress or change.
Past success = Future success
This is probably the most deeply rooted assumption and the most difficult to deconstruct. Track record is treated as a predictor of future behaviour without analysing the context in which that track record was built. A CFO who tripled margins in a stable market may lack the cognitive tools to navigate an expansion into volatile markets.
References = Reliability
Positive references are treated as a reliable signal of future performance. They are not, for two structural reasons: references are almost always provided by people selected by the candidate, and those who give negative references face reputational risk. The reference system is structurally designed to produce signals distorted toward the positive.

The problem is not the quantity of data. It is the quality of the questions.

The conventional organisational response to decision risk is the accumulation of information. Interview rounds are increased, psychometric tests are added, references are multiplied. The belief is that more data necessarily produces a better decision.

This belief is itself an assumption — and for many high-complexity contexts, it is a wrong one.

Research on decision-making under conditions of complexity reveals a counterintuitive phenomenon: beyond a critical threshold, the accumulation of information does not improve decision quality. It increases the confidence of decision-makers — who develop growing trust in their own assessment — without proportionally increasing the accuracy of that assessment. More data, same decision quality, more confidence. It is a particularly dangerous combination.

The problem is not the quantity of data collected. The problem is the quality of the questions guiding the collection and interpretation of that data. And the quality of questions depends on the ability to make explicit the assumptions governing the decision-making process.

An organisation that collects a hundred data points about an executive candidate while operating on unverified assumptions will collect a hundred confirmations of those assumptions. An organisation that first identifies its assumptions and subjects them to explicit verification can make a better decision with thirty data points collected in the right way.

The real work of Decision Intelligence: making the invisible visible

Decision Intelligence is not a tool for collecting more information. It is a discipline for building awareness of the cognitive structures that govern the interpretation of information already available.

In operational terms, this means introducing into the decision process a phase that is rarely made explicit: assumption mapping. Before collecting data, before evaluating candidates, before building an assessment framework, the question is asked: what are the implicit beliefs on which our reading of this problem rests?

This phase does not produce answers. It produces higher-quality questions. And it is higher-quality questions that make it possible to build more reliable decision processes.

The assumption mapping process follows a three-phase logic. The first is externalisation: assumptions are made explicit — written, formulated precisely, shared among participants in the decision process. This step, which appears simple, is in reality the most difficult, because it requires surfacing beliefs that the cognitive system manages automatically.

The second is classification: assumptions are categorised according to their impact on the decision and their verifiability. Some assumptions are marginal — even if wrong, they do not significantly change the decision. Others are foundational — if wrong, they completely overturn the assessment. It is on the latter that verification effort is concentrated.

The third is explicit verification: for each critical assumption, the specific evidence that would confirm or disconfirm it is identified, and that evidence is collected systematically. The data collection process thus becomes guided by the assumptions to be verified, not by a generic template.

Decision Stress Test

Before the next strategic decision

Ask yourselves these questions. Not after collecting data — before you begin collecting it.

→ What are the three main assumptions on which our reading of this problem rests?
→ What evidence could demonstrate that each of these assumptions is wrong?
→ If the most important assumption proved false, would it change our decision?
→ Who in our process has both the incentive and the legitimacy to challenge the dominant assumptions?
→ Were our assumptions built on contexts that remain relevant, or has the context changed?

If you cannot answer these questions with precision, the decision process already has a critical risk point — regardless of how much information has been collected.

This is not a data problem. It is a cognitive architecture problem.

A real case: when a perfect CV conceals a wrong assumption

A mid-sized organisation undergoing digital transformation seeks a new CFO. The ideal profile is built around an implicit assumption that is never explicitly formulated: that the right CFO for this phase is someone with deep financial management experience in mature, structured organisations.

The selected candidate has an impeccable CV on these parameters: fifteen years in senior finance roles at companies with revenues exceeding one billion, two managed IPOs, three rounds of structural cost optimisation. References are excellent. Interviews produce very positive impressions. The decision forms quickly and with broad consensus.

Sixteen months later, the organisation has a CFO who excels at reporting, compliance and banking relationships — skills of great value in a mature organisation. But the digital transformation requires something structurally different: the ability to build business cases for high-uncertainty investments, cognitive tolerance for the ambiguity of financial data in early phases, comfort with the speed of decision that technology cycles demand. These dimensions were not in the profile. Not because anyone had assessed them and deemed them secondary. Because the implicit assumption — seasoned CFO for a seasoned organisation — had rendered the right question invisible.

The assumption was not unreasonable. It was simply unverified. And the cost of not making it explicit had been transferred to the organisation.

The difference between those who absorb risk and those who manage it

There is a systematic difference between organisations that consistently produce high-quality decisions and those that generate costly errors with frequency. This difference does not reside primarily in the analytical capabilities available, nor in the quality of data accessed. It resides in an epistemic practice: the ability to identify and explicitly verify the assumptions governing the decision-making process.

Organisations that actively manage their decision risk have developed formal or informal mechanisms to do precisely this. Some do it through dedicated roles — figures with an explicit mandate to challenge the dominant assumptions in the process. Others do it through structured practices: pre-mortems in which participants are asked to imagine the decision has proved wrong and to identify the assumptions that would have led to that outcome. Others do it through explicit documentation of assumptions in decision minutes, which are later reviewed ex-post to assess the quality of reasoning — not just the outcome.

What these approaches share is a fundamental awareness: risk does not reside in the data that is missing. It resides in the beliefs one does not know one holds.

Organisations that have not developed this awareness are not necessarily less intelligent or less analytical. They have simply remained within a conception of risk that identifies the problem as an information problem, when it is structurally an interpretation problem.
