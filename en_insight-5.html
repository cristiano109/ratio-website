The Most Costly Hiring Mistake Happens Before the Interview

Organisations often attribute hiring failures to the wrong candidate. In reality, many decisions fail long before the candidate enters the room.

The mistake precedes the interview.

There is a widely held belief in organisations: when a hire produces disappointing results, the problem is the candidate. It was the wrong person. They weren't up to it. They weren't what they appeared to be.

This interpretation is convenient. It contains the damage, identifies an external party to blame, and leaves intact the confidence in the process that produced the choice. But it is almost always incomplete. And often entirely wrong.

The reality that emerges from analysing executive failures is far less comforting: the majority of high-impact hiring errors do not originate during the selection process. They originate before it. Before the organisation has met any candidate, written any job posting, or opened a single interview.

They originate from a poorly defined decision.

The myth of the perfect candidate

Organisational thinking on executive hiring is dominated by a search logic: find the right profile. This logic produces massive investment in gathering information about the candidate — CVs, references, tests, structured and unstructured interviews, psychometric assessments.

The implicit assumption underlying this approach is that a correct candidate exists, and that the organisation's task is to identify them with sufficient precision.

The problem with this assumption is that it shifts all decisional attention onto the object of the choice — the person — while overlooking the context in which that person will need to operate. And in executive hiring, context is not secondary. It is determinative.

A CFO who delivered exceptional results in a structured multinational may generate permanent friction in an organisation in expansion phase that requires decisional speed and tolerance for ambiguity. A Chief Revenue Officer capable of building complex pipelines in established markets may prove ineffective in a context that requires building a market from scratch. Not because these individuals are inadequate. But because the organisation had not defined with sufficient precision what problem needed to be solved, in which context, with which constraints.

Realistic scenario

A fast-growing company opens a search for a COO. The job description outlines consolidated operational competencies, experience in complex environments, ability to manage large teams. Candidates with impeccable profiles on these parameters are shortlisted. Six months after the hire, systemic friction emerges: the individual operates with efficiency and standardisation logic in a context that still requires flexibility and building processes from scratch. The decision to proceed with replacement comes four months later. The direct cost of replacement is recorded. Everything else — the delays in critical operational decisions, the loss of two key figures who found inadequate leadership, the growth opportunities missed during the transition — appears in no report. Yet it is permanent.

The job description had not described the real problem. It had described an ideal profile in the abstract.

The invisible origin of hiring errors

If one analyses executive hires that produced results significantly below expectations, a recurring pattern emerges. It is not an incorrect evaluation of the candidate during the interview. It is a structural problem that precedes the interview by weeks, sometimes months.

Organisations tend to define executive roles by accumulation: collecting expectations from those who held the role previously, adding current priorities, integrating requests from the functions involved. The result is often a job description that depicts a composite profile — someone with a veteran's experience, a startup's flexibility, a strategist's vision and an operator's pragmatism. A profile that rarely exists, and that even if it did exist would not solve the real problem, because the real problem was never explicitly articulated.

There are four structural absences that recur in selection processes that produce costly errors.

The first is the absence of a precise definition of the problem the role must solve. Not the role's responsibilities — the specific problem the organisation expects that person to resolve in the next eighteen to twenty-four months. This distinction may seem subtle, but it produces radically different evaluation criteria.

The second is the absence of measurable success indicators. How do we define this person's success at twelve months? At twenty-four? In what terms — not generic terms like "built an effective team" or "contributed to growth", but precise, observable, comparable terms.

The third is the absence of a map of the critical decisions associated with the role. What are the high-impact decisions this person will need to make? In what context? With what degree of autonomy? With what level of informational ambiguity? These questions are not secondary to candidate evaluation. They are its core.

The fourth is the absence of an explicit risk definition. What happens if this person doesn't work out? What is the cost of failure in terms of time, relationships, missed opportunities? Defining risk is not organisational pessimism. It is the rational basis for calibrating the intensity of the selection process.

When the interview arrives too late

The interview is the tool on which nearly all attention is concentrated in selection processes. It is prepared, structured, evaluated. Post-interview feedback is the primary decisional input. The impression formed from direct contact with the candidate is often what ultimately tips the scales.

This would not be a problem if the interview were preceded by a clear definition of what is being evaluated. But in most cases it is not. The interview arrives at a poorly defined process, and at that point it is asked to perform a function for which it was not designed: compensating for the lack of clarity upstream.

When an interview is called to do this work, it systematically produces distortions. The interviewer unconsciously seeks confirmation of an impression already formed in earlier stages — a form of confirmation bias that is particularly insidious because it masquerades as objective evaluation. Candidates with high communicative ability and brilliant self-presentation tend to be overestimated. Weak risk signals — an evasive response about a career transition, a certain difficulty in articulating significant failures, a narrative coherence that feels too constructed — are undervalued or ignored.

The interview, under these conditions, does not produce an evaluation. It produces a rationalisation of a decision that had already been made in implicit forms.

Decision Stress Test

Before the next executive interview

Answer these questions. Not during the interview — before meeting any candidate.

→ What specific problem must this role solve in the next 18 months?
→ What will be the first three critical decisions this person will need to make?
→ How will we measure success at 12 months? With which precise indicators?
→ What is the organisational cost of failure in this role?

If the answers are not shared and documented before the selection begins, the interview cannot compensate for that absence.

This is not a selection problem. It is a definition problem.

The best organisations start elsewhere

A systematic difference exists between organisations that produce high-quality executive hires and those that generate costly errors with regularity. This difference does not reside primarily in the quality of the selection tools used — assessments, assessment centres, panel interviews. It resides in what happens before any tool is activated.

Organisations with the best track records in executive hiring begin every selection with a phase that rarely appears in HR manuals: the definition of the role's decisional context. This phase involves the principal internal stakeholders — not to collect a list of desired characteristics in the ideal candidate, but to answer structurally different questions.

What organisational transformation must this role accompany? What are the internal tensions the new leader will need to navigate? What high-impact decisions await the person entering this role in their first six months? What capacity for managing uncertainty is required — and in what specific form?

Only once this context has been defined with sufficient precision is the evaluation framework built. And the framework is not a list of competencies. It is a map of the role's decisional requirements: the cognitive, behavioural and relational characteristics that decision-making research associates with sustained performance in that type of context.

This approach has a direct consequence on the interview's effectiveness: instead of being a generic profile-evaluation tool, the interview becomes a specific instrument for verifying precise hypotheses. How did the candidate manage situations analogous to the expected challenges — not generically difficult situations, but structurally similar ones? How is the quality of reasoning under the kind of pressure this role will generate — not the ability to appear composed in a comfortable interview setting?

Two decisional architectures compared

APPROACH A
Profile-based definition
The ideal candidate profile is constructed. People who match it are sought. The interview verifies adherence to the profile.
RESULT A
Decision based on impressions
High variance. Systematic biases. The best interview performer is not necessarily the most suitable candidate for the real context.
APPROACH B
Decisional context definition
The real problem, critical decisions, success indicators and risk are mapped. Evaluation verifies the capacity to navigate that specific context.
RESULT B
Defensible decision
Explicit, documentable, verifiable criteria. The final choice is justifiable to the board — and to oneself in the months that follow.

Decision quality precedes candidate quality

There is a conceptual shift that distinguishes organisations that understand decisional risk in executive hiring from those that merely absorb it: the awareness that the quality of a selection process output depends first on the quality of the decision that structures that process, and only secondarily on the quality of the tools used to evaluate candidates.

A selection process that is poorly structured upstream will produce unsatisfactory results regardless of the quality of assessments, the depth of references, the accuracy of interviews. Because all these tools are measuring something that was defined imprecisely or incorrectly.

Conversely, a process built on a precise definition of the problem, success indicators and the role's decisional profile transforms those same tools into reliable information vectors. The structured interview produces genuinely useful data. References are explored in a targeted manner. Assessment is calibrated to the real context, not to an abstract ideal profile.

The distinction between these two approaches is not technical. It is epistemic: it concerns the quality of the questions the organisation asks itself before beginning to evaluate the candidates' answers.
